Assignment 1 – Interactive Experience Review

Sculpting Harmony: Reviewing the Gehry/Getty Interactive Experience

A written interaction analysis of Sculpting Harmony: Frank Gehry and the Walt Disney Concert Hall

Name: Suzhe Wen Course: OART1013

Introduction

The interactive experience selected for this review is Sculpting Harmony: Frank Gehry and the Walt Disney Concert Hall, developed by Getty. Rather than functioning as a conventional website, the project presents itself as an immersive digital exhibition that combines scrolling, motion, image transformation, and architectural storytelling. My review focuses on how the experience guides attention, structures user behaviour, and communicates its goals through interaction design.

1. What was the first thing I paid attention to?

The first element that drew my attention was the immersive visual environment and the absence of a conventional interface. Rather than presenting itself as a typical website with menus and navigation bars, the experience immediately foregrounds large-scale imagery, motion, and spatial transitions. This creates the impression of entering a designed environment rather than browsing a webpage, encouraging a more exploratory mode of engagement.

2. My discrete actions during two minutes of interaction

During my initial interaction, I engaged primarily through scrolling, which triggered visual transitions and narrative progression. I paused at various moments to observe architectural models and shifting perspectives. I also moved the cursor across the screen to explore subtle interactive responses, although these were minimal. My actions were largely continuous and fluid, rather than discrete or segmented.

3. What part of the experience did I spend the most time engaging with?

I spent the most time engaging with sections that presented layered visual transformations of architectural forms. These moments, where models evolved or shifted in response to scrolling, required more attention and invited deeper observation. The gradual unfolding of these visuals created a sense of temporal immersion that encouraged prolonged engagement.

4. What was the most common action in my interaction?

The most common action was scrolling. Unlike traditional websites where clicking dominates interaction, scrolling in this experience functions as both navigation and narrative control. It becomes the primary mechanism through which the user advances the story and experiences the transformation of space.

5. What is my impression of the intended primary goal?

The intended primary goal of the experience appears to be the communication of architectural thinking as a dynamic and evolving process. Rather than presenting finished buildings as static objects, the project aims to simulate the development of form, material, and structure over time, allowing users to engage with architecture as an experiential narrative.

6. How does the interactive experience communicate this primary goal?

This goal is communicated through the integration of scrolling-based interaction with continuous visual transformation. As users scroll, architectural elements shift, assemble, and reconfigure, mirroring the iterative nature of design. The absence of explicit instructions reinforces an intuitive engagement, where understanding emerges through experience rather than explanation.

7. How should the experience be interacted with over time?

The experience is designed to be engaged with slowly and continuously. Rather than encouraging quick navigation, it invites sustained attention and deliberate pacing. Users are expected to move through the content gradually, allowing time for visual and spatial changes to unfold. It may also benefit from repeated viewing, as subtle details can be easily missed during a single interaction.

8. How is this communicated?

The pacing is communicated through smooth transitions and the lack of abrupt interaction points. The design avoids clickable elements and instead relies on continuous motion, which subtly signals that the experience should be navigated in a linear and uninterrupted manner. The visual rhythm encourages a slower, more contemplative interaction style.

9. What other media forms does the experience reference?

The experience references multiple media forms, including film, animation, and architectural visualisation. The use of camera-like movements and transitions evokes cinematic language, while the layered rendering of forms reflects techniques commonly found in 3D modelling and digital design software. These references contribute to a hybrid media experience that merges disciplines.

10. What do these references communicate about how I should act?

These media references suggest that the user should engage with the experience as a viewer rather than an active manipulator. The cinematic qualities position the user as an observer of unfolding events, encouraging passive exploration rather than direct control. This shapes behaviour by limiting interaction to navigation rather than intervention.

11. What do these references communicate about how I should feel?

The experience communicates a sense of contemplation and immersion. The slow transitions and abstract representations of architectural form create a reflective atmosphere, encouraging the user to focus on process and transformation. At times, the scale and fluidity of the visuals can also evoke a sense of awe or disorientation.

12. What was the most frustrating part?

The most frustrating aspect of the experience is the limited level of user agency. While the visual design is highly engaging, the lack of interactive control beyond scrolling can make the experience feel restrictive. Users who expect more direct interaction may find this passivity limiting.

13. What was the most satisfying part?

The most satisfying aspect is the seamless integration of interaction and narrative. The way in which scrolling directly influences the transformation of architectural forms creates a strong sense of connection between user input and visual output. This coherence enhances immersion and reinforces the conceptual strength of the experience.
